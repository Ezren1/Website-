Questions from Sectarians

Common objections and honest answers from a Qur'an-alone perspective, providing clarity and dispelling misconceptions.

Q: Are all hadiths false?

A: No, we do not claim that every Hadith narration is false in its entirety. However, the crucial point is that **the Qur’an never instructs us to rely on any collection of Hadith** for religious law or guidance beyond its own verses. The truth of Islam does not depend on fallible human narrations to survive or be understood. **Only the Qur’an is protected by Allah Himself.** Allah clearly states in the Qur'an that He is the sole guardian of His Reminder (15:9), referring exclusively to the Qur'an. Relying on Hadith, which were compiled centuries after the Prophet and are demonstrably full of contradictions, internal inconsistencies, and conflicts with the Qur'an, is a profound risk to one's faith.

Q: How do we pray without hadith?

A: The Qur’an commands prayer numerous times, gives its timings (e.g., 2:238, 11:114, 17:78, 24:58), and clearly shows its purpose: for Allah's remembrance (20:14) and to prevent evil and wrongdoing (29:45). While the Qur'an outlines key components like bowing (Ruku') and prostration (Sujud) and directs us to face the Sacred Mosque (2:144, 149-150), it does not prescribe a rigid, ritualistic step-by-step procedure. **Practices like the general form of standing, bowing, and prostrating have been passed down through generations of Muslims, originating from Prophet Ibrahim (a.s.)** and reinforced by Prophet Muhammad (PBUH). These universal practices, as long as they do not contradict the Qur’an, may be followed, but we do not treat them as divine law or a "second revelation." Our guidance for the specifics and the spirit of prayer comes directly from Allah's Book, encouraging sincere devotion over rigid ritualism.

Q: Isn’t this a new sect?

A: Absolutely not — this is, in fact, a **return to the original Islam** as revealed directly by Allah through the Qur’an. It means following Allah’s Book alone, without additions, innovations, or the divisions that have plagued the Muslim community for centuries. The Qur'an itself vigorously warns against forming sects and divisions: 'Indeed, those who have divided their religion and become sects - you [O Muhammad] are not [associated] with them in anything.' (Qur’an 6:159) and 'And be not among the polytheists, [among] those who have divided their religion and become sects, each faction rejoicing in what it has.' (Qur’an 30:31–32) By adhering solely to the Qur'an, we aim to transcend sectarian labels and unite on the single, pure source of divine truth provided by Allah, just as the early Muslims were united before the compilation of Hadith led to fragmentation.

Q: What about the Sunnah of the Prophet?

A: The **true Sunnah of the Prophet (his practice, his way)** was to follow the Qur'an perfectly and to live by its teachings. He was the prime example of a Qur'an-alone Muslim. Allah clearly states in the Qur'an that Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) was nothing more than a human messenger who delivered the revelation: 'Say, "I am only a human being like you, to whom has been revealed that your God is one God."' (Qur’an 18:110) and 'Nor does he speak from [his own] inclination. It is not but a revelation revealed.' (Qur’an 53:3-4) Therefore, his example, his "Sunnah," is found **within the Qur'an itself**, in how it describes a righteous messenger and how it commands believers to live. The Qur'an is the complete and perfect model, detailing how a messenger of Allah lives, teaches, and guides, making any external compilations of 'Sunnah' unnecessary and potentially problematic if they contradict the Qur'an.

Q: Doesn't the Qur'an mention 'Hadith'? So why reject it?

A: This is a crucial point of confusion. The word 'Hadith' (حديث) appears in the Qur'an in its literal Arabic meaning: **'statement,' 'narrative,' 'talk,' or 'discourse.'** For example, in Qur'an 45:6, it asks: "Then in which Hadith (statement/narrative) after Allah and His verses do they believe?" Here, 'Hadith' refers to any discourse that claims authority. The Qur'an uses 'Hadith' to refer to various types of narratives, including Allah's own revelations (e.g., 39:23 - 'Allah has sent down the best Hadith'), human talk (e.g., 18:6 - 'Perhaps you would kill yourself through grief over their [disbelief] if they do not believe in this Hadith'), or even fabricated stories (e.g., 12:111 - 'There was certainly in their stories a lesson for those of understanding. Never was it a fabricated Hadith'). **The Qur'an never uses 'Hadith' to refer to a specific, canonical collection of sayings attributed to the Prophet to be followed as religious law alongside itself.** The traditional Hadith collections (Bukhari, Muslim, etc.) are human compilations, not the 'Hadith' referred to in the Qur'an as divine guidance. The Qur'an warns against seeking any Hadith *other than Allah's revelation* for guidance, making it clear that Allah's Book is the only divinely authorized 'Hadith' to be followed.

✅ Fast During Ramadan

✅ Perform Hajj/Umrah

Complete the Hajj and Umrah for Allah (2:196). The Qur'an outlines the fundamental rites of pilgrimage, emphasizing devotion to Allah alone.

Following the Messenger means following the Qur'an.
